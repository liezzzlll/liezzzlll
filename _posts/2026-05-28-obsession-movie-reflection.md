**Spoilers ahead.**

I just got home from watching *Obsession*, and holy shit, that was such a work of art. I needed somewhere to put my immediate post-movie thoughts, so here we are. 

A lil _essay_. 

A lil vent.

Immediately after the movie, I called my friend who had already seen it. She pointed out the prevalence of mirrors, how often they appear throughout the film. We were talking so fast we kept talking over each other. Girl, we reached flow state lololol.

The more we talked, the more beautiful and disturbing it became. Wish-Nikki is Bear’s loneliness reflected back at him. 

## Post-Wish Nikki as Bear’s Mirror

Wish-Nikki mirrors Bear's emotional immaturity. She loves like someone in high school because Bear loves like someone who never learned how to move past crush-as-possession. She becomes melodramatic, panicked, clingy, and all-consuming because that is what his desire looks like underneath the shy-boy, good-guy packaging.

Post-wish Nikki is Bear’s fantasy.

Did you notice how cat-like Nikki acts in the beginning? Hiding in the corner. Peeking from behind the flower vase. Moving through the room in a strange, skittish manner. Perhaps that similarity paints the picture that Bear’s cat becomes the template for the only kind of love he seems to understand: dependent, physically affectionate, predictable, contained.

A pet can curl up beside you and ask very little of your inner world. A pet can need you in a way that feels simple. A pet can love you in a way that does not require equal emotional reciprocity, hard conversations, or the terrifying possibility of rejection.

So when Nikki becomes “loving,” she becomes cat-like, clingy, impulsive, physically attached, and emotionally mirrored. The wish prints Bear’s stunted idea of love onto Nikki’s body.

That is the horror. Nikki’s body becomes the physical manifestation for Bear’s emotional limitations, his loneliness gets a face. His fantasy becomes reality. I mean, the billion dollars did literally fall out of the sky for Ian, so we know the wish delivers!

And that makes it even scarier...

I believe our ability to love often reflects the depth of our own self-understanding, then the way wish-Nikki shows love reveals how Bear knows how to give and receive it. His version of love is intense, unstable, codependent, and consuming.

That hunger feels connected to something much larger than Bear alone. A lack of emotional fluency, unhealed wounds, repressed pain, and the inability to know oneself deeply can all feed what people often refer to as the male loneliness epidemic. Bear’s loneliness is painful and real, and I do not want to dismiss that. Loneliness deserves compassion. It becomes dangerous when it turns into entitlement, when someone starts treating another person’s attention, affection, or body as the cure for their own emptiness.

Bear mistakes the very human desire to be soothed from loneliness for love, instead of recognizing it as an invitation to sit with, name, and heal his own emotional discomfort. That is where the horror sharpens. His loneliness stops being an internal wound and becomes something imposed onto someone else.

## The Lonely Male Epidemic Trope

I’m not dismissing the external factors here. Bear is lonely. He is awkward, grieving, insecure, and socially fragile. His cat dies. He is emotionally isolated. He seems harmless at first in that soft, wounded, “I just want someone to love me, I'm a nice guy!” kind of way.

That familiarity is what makes him uncomfortable to watch.

Bear represents many aspects of the [lonely male epidemic trope](https://therepublicofletters.substack.com/p/why-the-male-loneliness-epidemic) because his loneliness is real. Loneliness is not inherently monstrous. It is a human wound. The problem begins when loneliness becomes a justification for entitlement, when emotional isolation turns into the belief that someone else should be responsible for repairing what you have not learned how to face.

The film slowly peels back the difference between loneliness as pain and loneliness as permission.

His sadness explains him. It does not excuse him.

Bear’s loneliness becomes dangerous when he starts treating Nikki’s autonomy as negotiable. He wants Nikki, and he mistakes wanting for love. He wants closeness, and he mistakes control for intimacy. He wants to feel chosen, so he removes Nikki’s ability to choose.

This is where the “lonely male epidemic” reading becomes sharper. The broader conversation around male loneliness often points to a real crisis of social disconnection, emotional isolation, and lack of intimate support. The U.S. Surgeon General has described [loneliness](https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/surgeon-general-social-connection-advisory.pdf) and isolation as a serious public health concern, with social disconnection affecting mental, physical, and community well-being. 

But *Obsession* explores what happens when loneliness is processed through entitlement rather than self-awareness. Bear does not build community. He does not practice honesty. He does not risk emotional intimacy in a mutual way. He places the full weight of his unmet needs onto Nikki and calls that love.

Nikki’s loss of bodily autonomy is not enough to make him fully reckon with what he has done. Her distress is not enough. Her altered behavior is not enough. Her suffering only becomes impossible for him to ignore once it starts damaging his world: his comfort, his friendships, his safety.

That is one of the cruelest truths in the movie. Nikki’s suffering should have been enough.

Bear shows us a deeply uncomfortable reality: some people do not actually want to be loved by another person. **They want to be rescued from the possibility of not being loved back.** They want love to arrive as relief from themselves, rather than as a relationship with another fully autonomous person.

That is why the lonely male trope is at play here. Bear’s loneliness and social isolation is the emotional condition that allows him to confuse need with love, projection with intimacy, and possession with connection. His isolation makes him sympathetic at first, then reveals how dangerous sympathy becomes when it is used to avoid accountability.

And when that fear goes unchecked, it can start calling obsession love.

## Running Away From Vulnerability

Bear cannot tell Nikki how he feels because that would require him to face the possibility of rejection. It would require him to sit in the discomfort of being seen and possibly not chosen. It would require him to let Nikki remain a person with her own choice.

He may not have known the wish would work, but the wish still reveals the impulse: he does not wish for courage, honesty, or the ability to be vulnerable. He wishes for Nikki to change. He wants love to happen to him through her, rather than through his own emotional risk.

That is what makes his connection with Sarah so important. Bear is able to be more honest with Sarah because she is not carrying the fantasy weight that Nikki carries. He tells Sarah about his cat at the bar before he ever tells Nikki. He does not even tell Nikki over the phone that he is grieving, even though that would be a completely understandable reason to miss trivia. He does not seem especially invested in trivia itself. He seems invested in being near Nikki.

Sarah reveals the kind of connection Bear is capable of accessing when he is not consumed by projection. With her, closeness feels more grounded and human. It is not built around obsession, fantasy, or the pressure of being chosen by the person he has placed on a pedestal. It asks for honesty, emotional risk, and the willingness to be seen without controlling the outcome. That is the path Bear keeps avoiding.

Vulnerability requires giving up control. It means telling the truth without managing the outcome. It means allowing another person to receive you, reject you, misunderstand you, or love you freely. Bear cannot tolerate that uncertainty, so he reaches for a wish that removes choice from the equation.

By the time Bear realizes the wish has become monstrous, his instinct is still rooted in control. He does not immediately try to free Nikki. He tries to alter the wish. He wants a more manageable version of the fantasy. A version with fewer consequences. A version where he can keep the love without confronting the violence that made it possible.

## Conclusion

The ending is disturbing because Nikki survives.

Bear’s death breaks the spell, but it does not undo the harm. Nikki returns to herself carrying the trauma of what happened and the horror of losing control over her own body. She survives, and survival does not make the violation disappear.

That part stayed with me.

When a woman’s bodily autonomy is taken from her, the burden does not disappear just because the immediate threat is gone. The spell ends. The body remembers.

Curry Barker has said the movie is interested in what it means to make someone love you without consent, and whether love can ever be real if it is not earned. That question sits at the center of the film.

Bear wanted Nikki to love him more than anyone in the world.

He never loved her enough to let her remain herself.

And that, to me, is the real horror of *Obsession*. 

Not the wish. 

Not the blood.

The real horror is how eerily it echoes what many survivors of sexual assault are forced to endure: the lack of consequences for the perpetrator, even when the survivor makes it out alive. Survival does not mean release. The harm remains with the person who was violated. Nikki is left carrying the burden, or rather, the Bear-den.
